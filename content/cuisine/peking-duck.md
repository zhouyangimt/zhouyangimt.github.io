![Peking Duck — glossy mahogany skin, carved tableside](/images/peking-duck-1.jpg)

The cart rolls up to your table. On it: a whole duck, skin the color of polished mahogany, glistening under the restaurant lights. The chef works fast — *thwack thwack thwack* — cleaving the skin off in neat rectangles, then slicing the meat into thin pinkish petals. The aroma hits you before the first piece touches the plate: malt sugar caramelized into glass, five-spice, wood smoke, roasted fat.

This is Peking Duck (北京烤鸭, *Běijīng kǎoyā*). It has been Beijing's greatest culinary export for over 600 years, and no trip to China's capital is complete without it.

## The Skin Is the Point

Let's be clear: Peking Duck is mostly about the skin. The meat is a bonus.

The skin should shatter. Literally shatter — like the top of a crème brûlée, but savory instead of sweet. Achieving this takes days. The duck is first pumped with air to separate skin from fat, then blanched, then hung to dry for 12–24 hours while a glaze of maltose syrup and vinegar sets. Only then does it go into the oven — traditionally a hung oven (挂炉) burning fruitwood like jujube or pear, which gives the skin that faint floral sweetness you can't quite place.

The result: lacquered, amber-brown skin that cracks between your teeth and dissolves into the richest, most concentrated duck flavor you'll ever taste.

## Two Schools of Carving

Beijing's duck establishment splits into two camps.

**Quanjude (全聚德)**, founded in 1864, slices each duck into exactly 108 pieces — skin and meat together, each piece with its proportional share of crackling. It's precision engineering applied to poultry.

**Bianyifang (便宜坊)**, founded in 1416 (yes, the Ming dynasty), uses a closed oven (焖炉) and a different philosophy: skin first, meat second, served in two separate courses. The skin comes out as pure golden shards. The meat follows in a stir-fry or soup.

Both are correct. Both are worth the flight.

## How to Assemble Your Wrap

The duck arrives with an armada of accompaniments:

- **Lotus-leaf pancakes** (荷叶饼) — steamed, paper-thin, the size of a CD
- **Sweet bean sauce** (甜面酱) — dark, glossy, fermented, spread on the pancake like butter
- **Scallion brushes** — white parts only, shredded into fine bristles
- **Cucumber matchsticks** — crisp, cold, the green counterpoint to all that richness

Now: lay the pancake flat. Smear a thumbprint of sweet bean sauce across the center. Place two or three pieces of duck (skin-side up, obviously) on the sauce. Add three scallion brushes, four cucumber slivers. Fold the bottom up, then roll from the side. One bite should give you crunch, chew, smoke, sweet, and vegetal snap all at once.

The first wrap is practice. The second is where the religion starts.

## The Imperial Roots

Peking Duck wasn't invented in a restaurant. It started in the imperial kitchens of the Yuan dynasty (1271–1368), refined through the Ming, and perfected under the Qing. Records from 1330 describe a roast duck recipe that already sounds familiar: the bird is scalded, dried, glazed, and roasted over a fire. By the time the Qianlong Emperor demanded it at every banquet, the dish had achieved mythical status.

When Quanjude opened its doors to the public in the late Qing dynasty, Peking Duck stopped being imperial and started being everyone's. Today, a proper Peking Duck dinner in Beijing runs about ¥200–400 per person — not cheap, but a fraction of what it would have cost an emperor.

## Don't Settle for the Imitation

Bad Peking Duck exists, and you've probably eaten it. The skin is flabby and pale. The meat tastes boiled. The pancakes are thick as tortillas and stick together in a gummy stack. The "hoisin" is actually just colored corn syrup.

Real Peking Duck has no shortcuts. The skin must be glossy and brittle. The pancakes must be translucent. The duck must be carved in front of you — because the skin loses its crackle within minutes of leaving the carcass. If they carve it in the kitchen and bring it out on a plate, send it back.

## One Duck, Two Courses

A proper Peking Duck dinner doesn't end with the wraps. The remaining carcass — with plenty of meat still clinging to the bones — goes back to the kitchen and returns as either:

- A milky-white **duck bone soup** (鸭架汤), boiled with tofu and napa cabbage until the broth turns opaque
- A **salt-and-pepper stir-fry** (椒盐鸭架) of the chopped frame, tossed with garlic, chilies, and Sichuan peppercorn salt

Both are excellent. Order both if the restaurant allows it. You're already committed.
