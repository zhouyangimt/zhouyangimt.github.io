This guide is about helping you do that.
## The Six Families
All tea — green, black, oolong, white, yellow, dark — comes from the same plant: *Camellia sinensis*. The difference between them is not the leaf. It's what happens to the leaf after it's picked. Specifically: how much it oxidizes.

Oxidation is the same process that turns an apple slice brown. When you crush or roll a tea leaf, its cell walls break open, enzymes meet oxygen, and the leaf's chemistry transforms. Stop the process early, you get green tea. Let it run, you get black tea. Control it somewhere in the middle, you get oolong. The six types are really six stops on a single spectrum.
## Green Tea (绿茶) — 0% Oxidation

<img src="/images/six-types-green.jpg" alt="Green Tea" loading="lazy">
The leaf is heated immediately after picking — pan-fired in China, steamed in Japan — to kill the enzymes before they can start their work. What you're tasting is the leaf itself, as close to alive as it gets.

**Taste**: Vegetal, nutty, marine. Think steamed edamame, roasted chestnuts, fresh-cut grass after rain. Zero bitterness when brewed right.

**Famous examples**: Longjing (Dragon Well), Biluochun, Huangshan Maofeng

**The one thing to remember**: Cool your water to 75-80°C. Boiling water is a green tea murder weapon.

## Black Tea (红茶) — 100% Oxidation

<img src="/images/six-types-black.jpg" alt="Black Tea" loading="lazy">
In China, this is called *hong cha* — "red tea," named for the color of the liquor, not the leaves. The leaves are fully oxidized before drying, which transforms the fresh, grassy polyphenols into deeper, sweeter compounds. This is what most of the Western world means by "black tea."

**Taste**: Malt, honey, cocoa, dried fruit. Smooth, no astringency. Takes milk well but doesn't need it.

**Famous examples**: Keemun (Qimen), Zhengshan Xiaozhong (Lapsang Souchong), Dianhong (Yunnan black)

**The one thing to remember**: Chinese black tea is naturally sweet. If you're adding sugar, the tea is bad or your water is wrong.

## Oolong Tea (乌龙茶) — 10-70% Oxidation

<img src="/images/six-types-oolong.jpg" alt="Oolong Tea" loading="lazy">
Oolong is the category that breaks the rules. It's partially oxidized — anywhere from a whisper (closer to green) to near-black (closer to red). The range is so wide that a light Tieguanyin and a dark Dahongpao taste like they come from different planets.

**Taste**: Floral, creamy, roasted, stone-fruit. Oolong is the perfume counter of Chinese tea. It smells like orchids and tastes like buttered toast.

**Famous examples**: Tieguanyin (Iron Goddess), Dahongpao (Big Red Robe), Fenghuang Dancong (Phoenix Single Bush)

**The one thing to remember**: Oolong is the only tea that genuinely gets better with multiple steeps. The third pour is often the best one.

## White Tea (白茶) — Minimal Processing

<img src="/images/six-types-white.jpg" alt="White Tea" loading="lazy">
White tea is tea doing as little as possible. The leaves are picked, withered in the sun, and gently dried. No rolling, no firing, no shaping. What you get is the leaf in its laziest, most honest form.

**Taste**: Honey, hay, melon, a faint floral sweetness that feels more like a suggestion than a statement. White tea whispers. You have to lean in.

**Famous examples**: Baihao Yinzhen (Silver Needle), Bai Mudan (White Peony), Shou Mei

**The one thing to remember**: White tea doesn't go bitter no matter how long you steep it. It's the most forgiving tea in existence — perfect for people who forget they're making tea.

## Yellow Tea (黄茶) — The Rare One

<img src="/images/six-types-yellow.jpg" alt="Yellow Tea" loading="lazy">
Yellow tea is green tea with one extra step: *men huang* (闷黄), "sealing yellow." After the kill-green heating, the leaves are wrapped in cloth and left to sweat gently. A tiny bit of non-enzymatic oxidation happens, softening the grassiness of green tea into something rounder and sweeter.

**Taste**: Sweeter than green tea, with a mellow, almost buttery finish. No grass. No bite.

**Famous examples**: Junshan Yinzhen, Huoshan Huangya, Mengding Huangya

**The one thing to remember**: Yellow tea is rare because it's hard to make well. The men huang step is temperamental — too much moisture, you get mold; too little, you get grassy green tea wearing a yellow name tag.

## Dark Tea (黑茶) — Fermented

<img src="/images/six-types-dark.jpg" alt="Dark Tea" loading="lazy">
Dark tea is the outlier. It doesn't just oxidize — it ferments. After initial processing, the leaves are piled, moistened, and left to host microbial activity that transforms the leaf chemistry over weeks, months, or decades. This is why pu'er cakes have vintages.

**Taste**: Earth, wood, old books, rain-wet stone. Dark tea tastes like a library basement smells — in the best possible way. It's warm, deep, and lingers for minutes after you swallow.

**Famous examples**: Shu Pu'er (Ripe), Sheng Pu'er (Raw), Liu Bao, Fu Zhuan

**The one thing to remember**: Rinse dark tea once with boiling water before your first real steep. It wakes the leaves up and washes off any storage dust.

## How to Start
If you've never bought Chinese tea before, here's the order I'd recommend:- **Start with oolong.** It's the crowd-pleaser — floral, sweet, forgiving. Tieguanyin is the entry point.
- **Try a Chinese black tea next.** It will ruin English Breakfast for you forever. Dianhong from Yunnan is the one to get.
- **Then explore green tea.** Longjing is the classic, but it's easy to overpay. Get the autumn harvest — 80% of the flavor at 30% of the price.
- **White and yellow when you're ready.** They're subtle. You need to have calibrated your palate first.
- **Dark tea last.** It's an acquired taste, like peated whisky or blue cheese. Some people fall in love immediately. Some need a year.
